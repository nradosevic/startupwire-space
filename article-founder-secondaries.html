The Rise of Founder Secondaries: When Should Entrepreneurs Take Money Off the Table?

📅 March 2, 2026 ✍️ Marcus Williams 📂 Business

A significant shift has occurred in how the startup ecosystem thinks about founder liquidity. Once considered taboo—a sign that founders weren't fully committed to their companies—secondary sales by entrepreneurs have become increasingly normalized and even encouraged. This change reflects a more mature understanding of founder incentives, risk management, and the realities of building companies over extended time horizons. For founders navigating this terrain, understanding the considerations around secondaries can inform better decision-making.

The traditional argument against founder secondaries was straightforward: founders should have maximum skin in the game, with all their financial upside tied to the company's success. Any reduction in that exposure might reduce motivation or signal lack of confidence to other stakeholders. This view still has adherents, particularly among investors who believe founder financial concentration creates beneficial alignment. However, the counterarguments have gained significant traction over the past few years.

Proponents of founder secondaries note that excessive financial concentration can actually impair decision-making. Founders with all their net worth in a single illiquid asset may become excessively risk-averse at precisely the moments when bold action is needed. They may push for premature exits or make operational decisions driven by personal financial anxiety rather than company optimization. Providing some liquidity can reduce this pressure and enable founders to focus on building rather than worrying about their personal financial situation.

The logistics of founder secondaries have become increasingly sophisticated. Dedicated secondary funds like Industry Ventures and EquityZen have built platforms specifically for these transactions. Many venture capital firms now include secondary components in their financing rounds, allowing founders to sell a portion of their holdings alongside the primary capital raise. The mechanics have become standardized, with well-understood structures for pricing, information rights, and transfer restrictions.

Timing considerations are critical when evaluating secondary sales. Most advisors suggest that secondaries make most sense after a company has achieved meaningful milestones—typically Series B or later—when valuation is more established and founders have demonstrated long-term commitment. Selling too early can send problematic signals and may also mean selling at valuations that don't reflect the company's true potential. The conventional wisdom suggests that founders should consider secondaries only after several years of committed building.

The amount founders should sell is highly personal and depends on individual circumstances. Some advisors suggest selling enough to eliminate any financial anxiety—often characterized as enough to buy a home or establish a comfortable safety net—while retaining substantial upside in the company's eventual outcome. Others recommend thinking about diversification as a percentage of net worth, targeting levels that would be considered prudent for any concentrated position. There's no universal answer, but selling between 10-20% of holdings during a secondary transaction has become relatively common.

Transparency with investors and employees is essential when contemplating secondary sales. Most sophisticated investors now understand and accept founder liquidity as part of the startup ecosystem, but surprises can damage trust. Founders should communicate their intentions openly and ensure that any secondary sale is structured in ways that don't conflict with company interests or create misalignment with other stakeholders. When handled thoughtfully, founder secondaries can actually strengthen relationships by demonstrating that the company has reached a stage of maturity and stability.
